## 2. CartiHeal Founding History

**CartiHeal Ltd.** was founded in approximately **2009** in Israel as a medical device startup focused on a novel approach to osteochondral defect repair. The company was incubated within the Israeli medtech ecosystem, which has produced numerous orthopedic device companies.

CartiHeal's core innovation was the **Agili-C implant**: a fully resorbable, biphasic (two-layer) scaffold made from aragonite (a naturally occurring form of calcium carbonate), designed to simultaneously regenerate both the cartilage and subchondral bone layers of an osteochondral defect. The aragonite scaffold is porous, allowing bone marrow-derived stem cells to migrate into the implant and differentiate into both chondrocytes (cartilage cells) and bone cells.

The company initially raised funding from Israeli and international medtech investors. **Bioventus**, a US-based orthobiologics company, made an early strategic investment in CartiHeal and held an option to acquire the company contingent on FDA approval.

A pivotal moment came in 2022 when the FDA granted **Premarket Approval (PMA)** for the Agili-C implant based on a landmark Level 1 randomized controlled trial — making Agili-C the first FDA-approved osteochondral scaffold for cartilage repair. Following FDA approval, Bioventus exercised its call option, acquiring CartiHeal for approximately **$315 million upfront** plus **$135 million contingent** on achieving $100 million in trailing 12-month sales.

However, Bioventus subsequently struggled with financial pressures and in **January 2024** sold CartiHeal to **Smith+Nephew** for approximately **$180 million upfront** plus up to **$150 million contingent** on future financial performance — a significant write-down from the Bioventus acquisition price, reflecting Agili-C's slower-than-anticipated commercial ramp.

Smith+Nephew completed the CartiHeal acquisition on **January 10, 2024**, integrating Agili-C into its Sports Medicine & ENT business unit.

|---|---|

## 7. Go-to-Market

**Target Customer Segments**:
- Orthopedic surgeons treating patients with knee cartilage defects, including those with mild-moderate OA
- Distinct from MACI's patient profile: Agili-C's label includes OA patients, dramatically expanding the addressable population

**Sales Motion**:
- Integration into Smith+Nephew's global Sports Medicine direct sales force — this is the primary commercial advantage over the standalone CartiHeal entity; S+N's existing surgeon relationships and hospital contracts accelerate adoption
- Demonstrated at AAOS 2024 as part of Sports Medicine portfolio alongside REGENETEN and other biological healing solutions

**Key Competitive Positioning vs. MACI**:
- Off-the-shelf: No two-stage procedure, no patient biopsy, immediate availability
- Single-stage: One operating room visit vs. two for MACI
- Broader OA indication: Addresses patients typically excluded from MACI
- Potential cost advantage: Scaffold device vs. autologous cell therapy manufacturing

**China VBP Headwind**: Smith+Nephew noted that Sports Medicine Joint Repair faced a headwind from China's Volume Based Procurement (VBP) program that commenced in May 2024. This reflects the geopolitical risk of China market exposure for premium orthopedic products.

## 8. Technology and IP

**Agili-C Technology**:
- Biphasic aragonite (calcium carbonate) scaffold: Upper phase promotes cartilage regeneration; lower phase promotes subchondral bone regeneration
- Porous architecture enables migration of bone marrow-derived mesenchymal stem cells into the implant
- Fully resorbable: Scaffold resorbs over time as regenerated tissue fills the defect
- No cells, proteins, or drugs — pure scaffold; simplifies regulatory status relative to cell therapies and reduces manufacturing complexity

**IP**:
- Patents on aragonite scaffold composition, design, and surgical technique; originally filed by CartiHeal; now held by Smith+Nephew

## 9. Competitive Positioning

**vs. Vericel MACI**:
- Agili-C is structurally simpler (device vs. biologic cell therapy), enabling single-stage procedure
- Agili-C addresses patients with mild-moderate OA — estimated 4–5x larger patient population than isolated defects treated by MACI
- MACI has longer established US commercial track record and stronger surgeon training network
- Head-to-head clinical comparison does not exist; different patient populations make direct comparison difficult

**vs. Zimmer DeNovo NT**:
- Both are off-the-shelf; Agili-C addresses osteochondral (full-thickness cartilage + bone) defects; DeNovo NT is pure cartilage (chondral) defect treatment
- Different but overlapping indications

**Key Risk**: The slower-than-expected commercial ramp that led Bioventus to sell CartiHeal at a loss suggests surgeon adoption of Agili-C has been gradual. Smith+Nephew's distribution network and AAOS presence should accelerate this, but the commercial execution risk is real and evidence-based.

## 10. Strategic Direction

**Integration Priority**: Following the January 2024 acquisition, Smith+Nephew's primary strategic imperative for Agili-C is commercial ramp — achieving the $150M contingent payment milestone (tied to revenue performance) would be a key internal target.

**Technology Integration**: Smith+Nephew has publicly discussed integrating CartiHeal with its robotics and digital surgery suite, positioning Agili-C as part of a comprehensive knee solution (robotics-assisted implantation of Agili-C); this is a potential differentiation lever in the premium hospital segment.

**Portfolio Direction**: Smith+Nephew is building a comprehensive "biological healing" Sports Medicine portfolio (Agili-C + REGENETEN + BMA systems), positioning itself as the leader in orthobiologics within sports medicine.

## 11. SWOT Summary

**Strengths**
- Only FDA PMA-approved osteochondral scaffold for cartilage repair in the US with Level 1 RCT evidence
- Broadest approved patient indication in cartilage biologics (includes mild-moderate OA)
- Off-the-shelf, single-stage procedure — logistically simpler than MACI
- 4-year data (published 2025): 87% lower risk of TKA or osteotomy — compelling long-term outcome data
- Smith+Nephew's global distribution network and surgeon relationships dramatically exceed standalone CartiHeal commercial capability

**Weaknesses**
- Commercial ramp was slower than anticipated under Bioventus — surgeon adoption has been gradual
- Revenue not broken out publicly — limits investor visibility into commercial trajectory
- Smith+Nephew's overall company is under operational pressure (group-wide transformation underway)
- China VBP headwind limits Asian market commercial contribution

**Opportunities**
- OA patient population is dramatically larger than isolated defect population — represents a potential step-change in addressable market if Agili-C achieves reimbursement for OA indication at scale
- Robotics integration with Smith+Nephew CORI system could create premium positioning
- International expansion (EU, APAC) where Agili-C may face less competitive pressure from MACI (US-only)
- Long-term data continues to accumulate (5-year, 7-year) strengthening clinical story

**Threats**
- Vericel MACI Arthro narrows the "less invasive" advantage of Agili-C in the non-OA patient segment
- Reimbursement coverage for OA indication may lag FDA label — payers may initially only cover isolated defect treatment
- If Smith+Nephew faces broader corporate distress (ongoing transformation challenges), Agili-C commercial investment could be deprioritized
- OrthoCellix NeoCart or future cell therapies may offer clinical superiority in non-OA patients
